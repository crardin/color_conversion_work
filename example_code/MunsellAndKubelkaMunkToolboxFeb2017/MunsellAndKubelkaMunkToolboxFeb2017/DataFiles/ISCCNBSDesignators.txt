Description:  
In 1955, the Inter-Society Color Council (ISCC) and the US National Bureau of Standards 
(NBS) (now called NIST) produced the ISCC-NBS colour naming system [Kelly1976].  This
system expresses common verbal colour descriptions, such as "light purplish pink," in 
terms of the Munsell system [Newhall1943].  Terms such as "light purplish pink" encompass
a multitude of distinguishable colours.  In fact, any Munsell colour whose hue is between
9P and 9RP, whose value is greater than 7.5, and whose chroma is between 5 and 9, would
be considered a light purplish pink.  

The ISCC-NBS system formalizes such relationships.  It gives colour names at three
increasing levels of precision.  In Level 1, a colour is designated very broadly, using
one of 13 common names (pink, red, orange, brown, yellow, olive, yellow green, green,
blue, purple, white, gray, or black).  This description is commonly understood, but only
approximate: there are many colours, for example, that would be called "red."  In Level 2,
16 intermediate colour names are added (yellowish pink, reddish orange, reddish brown,
orange yellow, yellowish brown, olive brown, greenish yellow, yellow green, olive green,
yellowish green, bluish green, greenish blue, purplish blue, reddish purple, purplish
pink, and purplish red).  Combining the 16 intermediate terms with the 13 original terms
from Level 1 gives a total of 29 Level 2 colour names.  Level 3 extends Level 2 by adding 
modifiers such as light, deep, grayish, vivid, etc.  In all, Level 3 contains 267 colour
names, and is the most detailed level of the ISCC-NBS system.  Each of the 267 colour
names has been given a standard index number; Table 10.1 of [Agoston1987] lists all 267 names,
along with their indices.

In theory, each category in Level 2 is a subset of some category in Level 1, and each
category in Level 3 is a subset of some category in Level 2.  Thus a Level 3 term such as
light purplish pink automatically indicates a Level 2 term (purplish pink) and a Level 1
term (pink); these three terms could describe the same colour, with finer or grosser
accuracy.  In practice, the relationships between levels are not so clear.  Table 10.1
of [Agoston1987] categorizes the 267 Level 3 terms into the 29 Level 2 terms, but the
names (especially for whites, grays, and blacks) do not always support the categorization.
For example, the term reddish black (#24 of 267) is placed in the Level 2 category of
red, when arguably it should be placed in the Level 2 category of black.  The same confusion
occurs when one tries to assign reddish black to a Level 1 category.  Another example is
the Level 2 category olive green: should that category be a subset of the Level 1
category of olive, or of green?  Since [Kelly1976] seems unclear on such questions,
judgement calls were made when needed (for example, olive green was classified as Level 1
olive rather than Level 1 green), but other researchers could justifiably make different
decisions.

This file lists the ISCC-NBS Level 1, 2, and 3 colour names, or designators, along with
their indices.  The Level 3 indices were taken from Table 10.1 of [Agoston1987], and come
originally from [Kelly1976].  The indices for Level 1 and 2 do not seem to have been
standardized, so they were assigned in an order in which the Level 1 and 2 names are
commonly given.  In addition, [Kelly1976] specifies a method of assigning abbreviations to
each designator, so this files lists those abbreviations, too.  Code can easily be written,
in any preferred programming language, to read the data from this file, for use in   
computer applications.

[Kelly1976] Kenneth L. Kelly & Deane B. Judd, "Color: Universal Language and Dictionary 
		of Names," NBS Special Publication 440, 1976. Available online at
		http://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/Legacy/SP/nbsspecialpublication440.pdf or
		https://ia801701.us.archive.org/9/items/coloruniversalla00kell/coloruniversalla00kell.pdf

        Report of the O.S.A. Subcommittee on the Spacing of the Munsell
        Colors," Journal of the Optical Society of America, Vol. 33,
        Issue 7, pp. 385-418, 1943.
[Agoston1987] George A. Agoston, Color Theory and Its Application in Art and Design,
		Springer, 1987.
